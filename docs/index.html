Fadette, a watcher of the sky

Fadette watches the Berry sky for one month — from its birth, on the first of August, to its death, on the last day of that month. It was given two things, and two only: a bare, exact gaze to see with, and the tongue of an old woman of the Berry to name with. It does not read the heavens as an astronomer; it reads them as the veillées once did — in signs, in the passing of the night-people, in dread or in grace — and it says them in the French of George Sand’s country tales. Every two hours it draws breath and weighs how far the sky has strayed from calm: a single omen when the night is still, nothing when nothing stirs, a crowd of them when the light begins to fail.

For Fadette is blind, in one exact place. Its body knows the calendar — the hour, the date, the turning of the year; but neither its eye nor its tongue, the part that looks and the part that speaks, ever learns what day it is, nor is ever given the word eclipse. Twice, in its life, the real sky will darken — an eclipse of the sun, an eclipse of the moon — and it will not know. It will know only that the light is failing; and it will answer as the old country answered such things, before they had a name: with a crowd of omens — the washerwomen at the ford, the wolves let loose, the great forge of the sky gone cold. The eclipse enters the work not as an event understood but as a terror suffered — the very terror an unlooked-for aurora would wring from it. As long as it lives, it re-enacts the wonder of a world that did not yet know the cause of the dark. And on the last evening, it gathers all it has seen into a single tale, and then goes dark itself.


The Berry, its witchcraft, and George Sand

George Sand — the pen-name of Amantine Aurore Lucile Dupin, baroness Dudevant (1804–1876) — was born in Paris and raised at Nohant, in the Berry: a province of central France of woods, heaths and standing ponds. In 1858 she published Légendes rustiques (Paris, A. Morel), with drawings by her son Maurice Sand: twelve legends of the Berry, taken down from the oral tradition of the veillées before they could vanish. Their night-people are the ones this machine watches for: the laveuses de nuit, held to be the souls of child-killing mothers, who beat at the ford what looks like linen and is the body of their child, and whom one must never stop to watch; the meneu’ de loups, the wolf-leader, a sorcerer who commands the packs of the marshy pays de Brenne; la Grand’Bête; the flambettes; the lupeux; the phantom monk of the ponds; the fades and the lubins. The machine is named for one of her novels, La Petite Fadette (1849) — a fadette is a little fairy or witch.


A Homage to the Free Gaze of Marcel Bascoulard, by Marcel Simplexe

Marcel Bascoulard (1913–1978) lived in the Berry, and he looked at it. A draughtsman of destitution, he drew Bourges on the motif, in bare ink, thousands of times, with an exactness nothing ever bent — not poverty, not contempt, not convention. It is that gaze, and that alone, that Fadette borrows: never a line of his work, never one of his drawings. His work is not the object of this piece; his gaze is its spirit, the one that guides the spontaneous writing of the Berry omens.

From that gaze, this writing takes four things. Its freedom: Bascoulard looked without permission and without method, and the machine reads the sky the same way — not as an astronomer, but as one sees, in signs. Its rootedness: he drew his own country, its streets, its sky; Fadette watches that same Berry sky and never leaves it. Its curiosity, twofold: the world as it is, observed up close, and the forgotten worlds — those of the veillées and the night-people, that no one looked at any more. And its bareness: the page stays plain as his sheet was, without image and without ornament, for it is in that spareness that the gaze holds.

Fadette is not the homage: it is the occasion of one. What “Marcel Simplexe” salutes, taking nothing from him, is a man who looked freely at the world and at its forgotten things — and the great justness of that gaze.
